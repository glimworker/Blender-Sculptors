Make It Real

Materials, Lighting & Rendering

Turn your sculpture into a finished image

What This Guide Is For

You've learned how to shape things in Blender. Now you'll learn how to make them look real.

This guide walks you through three finishing steps: giving your object a material (what it's made of), lighting it properly, and rendering a final image. We'll use a sphere as our subject — but everything here works on any sculpture you've made.

By the end of this guide you'll be able to:
  • Apply materials that look like real surfaces — shiny, matte, metallic, rough
  • Set up lighting that shows off your sculpture's form
  • Render a high-quality image you can print or share

The Question

You're about to model one of the balls you analysed. How perfect can you make it look?
A digital sphere is just flat faces pretending to be curved. Can you make the eye stop noticing?

Phase 1: Make It Smooth

Before materials or lighting — fix the surface

💡 Why This Matters

Every object in Blender is made of flat faces. On a sphere, you can see the facets unless you tell Blender to smooth them. This is the single biggest jump in realism.

📝 Step by Step

  1. Start with a good sphere: Add > Mesh > UV Sphere. In the bottom-left popup, increase Segments to 64 and Rings to 32 (more faces = smoother result)
  2. Apply Smooth Shading: Right-click the sphere > Shade Smooth. The facets disappear instantly
  3. Optional — Subdivision Surface modifier: In Properties panel > Modifiers (wrench icon) > Add Modifier > Subdivision Surface. Set Viewport level to 2, Render level to 3. This mathematically smooths the geometry further
  4. Check your work: Orbit around the sphere. Look at the silhouette edge — is it perfectly smooth? If you see any bumps, increase subdivision

⚠️ Common Mistake

If Shade Smooth makes your sculpted object look weird or blobby, try: Right-click > Shade Auto Smooth instead. This smooths curves but keeps intentional hard edges sharp. Perfect for objects that have both smooth surfaces and crisp details.

Phase 2: Give It a Material

What is your object made of?

💡 Why This Matters

A material tells Blender what the surface looks like: its colour, whether it's shiny or matte, rough or smooth, metallic or plastic. Without a material, everything is the same boring grey.

📝 Step by Step

  1. Select your object and go to the Material Properties tab (the sphere icon in the Properties panel on the right)
  2. Click "New" to create a new material. Give it a name that makes sense (e.g. "Red Leather", "Chrome", "Tennis Ball Felt")
  3. Switch viewport to Material Preview mode: Click the third sphere icon in the header bar (or press Z and select Material Preview). Now you can see your material live
  4. Set Base Color: Click the white rectangle next to "Base Color" and pick your colour. For realistic objects, avoid pure white or pure black — real things are always slightly off
  5. Set Roughness: This is the big one.
    • 0.0 = perfect mirror (chrome, wet surface)
    • 0.3–0.5 = slightly shiny (plastic, polished wood, new football)
    • 0.7–0.9 = matte/rough (chalk, concrete, old tennis ball)
    • 1.0 = completely matte (no reflections at all)
  6. Set Metallic:
    • 0.0 = non-metal (plastic, rubber, leather, fabric, wood — most things)
    • 1.0 = metal (steel, gold, aluminium, chrome)
    • There's no in-between in real life — something either is or isn't metal

💡 Pro Tips

  • Subsurface Scattering (under the material settings) makes things look slightly translucent — useful for wax, skin, or anything light passes through slightly. Try a value of 0.1–0.3 with a warm colour
  • Specular controls how bright the shiny highlight is. Default 0.5 is fine for most things. Lower it for very soft materials like cloth
  • Zoom in close and orbit while adjusting. Small changes in roughness make a big visual difference

Phase 3: Light It

Good lighting is the difference between amateur and professional

💡 Why This Matters

Lighting reveals form. A perfectly modelled sphere with bad lighting looks flat and boring. Good lighting shows every curve, every subtle surface detail, and creates mood.

📝 Option A: HDRI Environment Lighting (Easiest & Best Looking)

An HDRI is a 360-degree photograph of a real environment. Blender uses it to light your object as if it were actually sitting in that place.

  1. Go to World Properties (the globe icon in Properties panel)
  2. Click the yellow dot next to "Color" and select Environment Texture
  3. Click Open and load an HDRI file. Free HDRIs available at polyhaven.com — download any you like (try "studio small" for a clean product look, or "outdoor" for natural lighting)
  4. Switch viewport to Material Preview mode (Z > Material Preview) to see the result live
  5. Rotate the HDRI if needed: in World Properties, add a Mapping node and adjust the Z Rotation to move the light source around your object

📝 Option B: Three-Point Lighting (More Control)

The classic photography setup. Three lights, each doing a specific job.

  1. Delete the default light (select it, press X)
  2. Key Light (main light): Add > Light > Area Light. Position it above and to the left of your object at roughly 45 degrees. Set Power to around 100–200W. This is your main light source
  3. Fill Light (softens shadows): Add another Area Light. Position it to the right, slightly lower. Set Power to about half the key light (50–100W). This fills in the dark shadows so they're not pure black
  4. Rim Light (separates from background): Add a third Area Light behind the object, pointing toward the camera. Set Power similar to the fill. This creates a bright edge that makes the object "pop" off the background
  5. Adjust to taste: Move lights around, change their power. Use Material Preview to see changes live

🎨 Light Types Explained

  • Area Light: Soft, realistic shadows. Best for most situations. The bigger you scale it, the softer the shadows
  • Point Light: Like a bare bulb — light goes in all directions. Harder shadows
  • Sun Light: Parallel rays like actual sunlight. Good for outdoor scenes. Rotation matters, position doesn't
  • Spot Light: Focused cone of light. Good for dramatic, focused illumination

💡 Pro Tips

  • For a clean "product shot" look: one HDRI environment + one extra area light for a highlight
  • If shadows are too harsh, make your area lights bigger (scale them up)
  • A slightly coloured light adds realism — try a very faint warm yellow for the key light
  • Dark background + rim light = dramatic. Light background + soft fill = clean and modern

Phase 4: Render It

Produce your final image

💡 Why This Matters

Rendering takes everything — your model, material, and lighting — and calculates a final image. It's the difference between a preview and a finished photograph.

📝 Step by Step

  1. Position your camera: Select the Camera object. Press Numpad 0 to see through the camera. Use G then middle-mouse to fly it into position. Or: select your object, then Numpad . to centre it, then Cmd+Alt+Numpad 0 to snap the camera to your current view
  2. Choose your render engine: In Render Properties (camera icon):
    • EEVEE — Fast, looks good, great for quick results. Use this first
    • Cycles — Slower but physically accurate. More realistic reflections, shadows, and light. Use this for your final "best" render
  3. Set resolution: In Output Properties (printer icon), set Resolution X: 1920, Y: 1080 (or 1080 x 1080 for a square image). Set % to 100
  4. Render! Press F12 (or Render > Render Image). Wait for it to finish
  5. Save the image: In the render window: Image > Save As. Choose PNG format. Pick a sensible filename and location

⚙️ Render Settings That Matter

  • EEVEE tips: Turn on Screen Space Reflections (Render Properties > Screen Space Reflections > tick the box). Turn on Ambient Occlusion for subtle shadow in crevices. These two settings massively improve EEVEE quality
  • Cycles tips: Set Samples to 128 for a test render, 512–1024 for a final image. Enable Denoising (Render Properties > Denoising > tick) — this smooths out grain and lets you use fewer samples
  • Background: For a clean white background, go to World Properties and set the colour to white. For a transparent background (just the object, no background), go to Render Properties > Film > tick Transparent, then save as PNG (which supports transparency)

⚠️ Watch Out

  • Cycles renders can take several minutes — be patient, especially on older machines
  • If your render is completely black, you probably forgot to add lights (or your camera is inside the object!)
  • If it looks nothing like the viewport, make sure you're in Material Preview mode when setting up — Solid mode doesn't show materials or lighting
  • Save your .blend file before rendering. Always

Your Challenge: The Perfect Ball

Apply everything above to one task

🎯 The Task

Model the ball you analysed. Make it look as real as possible. Produce a rendered image that could fool someone into thinking it's a photograph.

📝 Workflow

  1. Research: Look at your ball closely. What is the surface actually like? Shiny? Textured? Are there seams, stitching, dimples, panels? Write down what you see
  2. Model: Create your sphere. Add subdivision. Apply Shade Smooth. If your ball has texture details (stitching, dimples), sculpt them using tools from the earlier tasks
  3. Material: Set up a material that matches. Use the recipes table above as a starting point, then adjust. Compare your screen to the real ball constantly
  4. Light: Choose HDRI or three-point. Match the kind of lighting you'd see in a real photo of your ball
  5. Camera: Frame it well. Try different angles. A slightly low angle with the ball filling most of the frame often works best
  6. Render: Do a quick EEVEE render first to check everything. Then switch to Cycles for your final image
  7. Evaluate: Hold the render next to a photo of the real ball. What's the gap? Could you close it further?

✨ Show & Tell

When you present your rendered ball, be ready to:

  • Show the real ball next to your render — how close did you get?
  • Explain what material settings you used and why
  • Describe what was hardest to get right
  • Answer the big question: is your digital sphere a perfect sphere?
